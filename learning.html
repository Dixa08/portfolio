LSRW Technique & Vocabulary

  • LSRW Method: Practice Listening, Speaking, Reading, and Writing daily.
  • Listening & Speaking: Listen to English audio, record your speech, and identify mistakes.
  • Reading & Writing: Read 3–4 pages daily and write your thoughts for better clarity.
  • Vocabulary (A–Z Method): Learn 5 new words daily → around 130 words in 26 days.
  • Application: Use new words in daily conversations; noticeable improvement in 4–5 weeks.

Key Learning: Continuous self-improvement, communication skills enhancement, and career-focused growth strategies.

Lab 4: Presentation Skills and Professional Behaviour

Focused on preparing, organizing, and delivering effective presentations. Learned body language, voice modulation, and audience engagement.

Introspective: Spending time examining one's own thoughts, ideas, and feelings.

Mockery: Insulting or contemptuous speech or behavior.

  • Who Gives Presentations: Students, teachers, employees, politicians, speakers, and leaders.
  • Why We Give Presentations: To inform, persuade, motivate, and endorse ideas.
  • Objectives of Presentation: What (topic), Why (purpose), Who (audience), along with engagement and action.
  • Start with a Hook: Use a question, fact, story, or light joke to grab audience attention.
  • Logical Sequence: Follow a clear order: WHO → WHAT → WHY for better understanding.
  • Content Style: Use bullet points, avoid long paragraphs, and keep content simple and clear.
  • Fonts & Colors: Use simple fonts like Arial/Calibri, proper size, bold headings, and a consistent theme.
  • Ethos, Pathos, Logos: Use credibility (ethos), emotions (pathos), and logical data like facts and statistics (logos).
  • Delivery Skills: Maintain eye contact, proper posture, effective gestures, confidence, and a smile.
  • Closing & Preparation: End with a summary and call to action; practice well to overcome stage fear.

Key Learning: Public speaking confidence, presentation structure, and professional communication skills.

Lab 5: Group Presentation and Team Collaboration

Lab 6: Communication Parameters

  • Grooming: Hair, makeup, accessories, wristwatch, formal footwear, and attire contribute to a professional appearance.
  • Verbal Communication: Tone, pitch, volume, pauses, voice modulation, and articulation help in delivering messages effectively.
  • Non-Verbal Communication: Eye contact, smile, gait (walking style), hand gestures, facial expressions, posture, and body language reflect confidence and clarity.
  • Content & Knowledge: Strong research, clear concepts, and use of ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic). Also includes answering key “WH” questions (what, why, how, when).
  • X-Factor: Confidence, personality, stage presence, and overall persona that make communication impactful and memorable.
  • Mehrabian Model of Communication: Suggests that communication is 7% words, 38% tone of voice, and 55% body language, highlighting the importance of non-verbal cues.
  • Theory of Instant Gratification: Refers to the desire for immediate results or rewards, which can affect communication style and patience in conversations.
  • Concept of Ikigai: A Japanese concept meaning "reason for being." It helps individuals align passion, mission, profession, and vocation for meaningful communication and life goals.

Formal Communication & CV / Cover Letter

  • Why Formal Communication is Required: Structured, professional, and well-maintained communication used in CVs, emails, and proposals.
  • Cover Letter: A formal document sent along with a CV while applying for a job.
  • Cover Letter Structure: Header (sender, date) → Receiver (name, company, position) → Opening → Body (skills, achievements) → Closing.
  • CV Structure: Personal Details → Education → Skills → Experience → Achievements.
  • Do’s & Don’ts:
    ✔ Formal language, clear format, correct grammar
    ✔ Short and relevant content
    ❌ Avoid mistakes, informal tone, unnecessary details

Key Learning:professional documentation skills
